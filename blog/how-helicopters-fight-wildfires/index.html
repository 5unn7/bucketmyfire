How wildfires are fought

How Helicopters Fight Wildfires in Canada

Published June 9, 2026

Key takeaways

  • Aircraft rarely extinguish a fire on their own. They buy time for ground crews and keep flames off communities (Natural Resources Canada).
  • Wildland fire management in Canada is a provincial and territorial responsibility; provinces decide whether and how to fight each fire (Natural Resources Canada).
  • CIFFC, owned by the federal, provincial, and territorial agencies, shifts crews, aircraft, and equipment between jurisdictions (Public Safety Canada).
  • Saskatchewan's SPSA owns 10 air tankers and seven bird-dog aircraft but no helicopters; it contracts helicopters from commercial carriers (Government of Saskatchewan).
  • A tanker group runs as a team of eight: five pilots, two maintenance engineers, and one air attack officer (Government of Saskatchewan).

What is aerial wildfire fighting?

Aerial wildfire fighting is the use of aircraft to find, slow, and put out forest fires. Helicopters and air tankers drop water or retardant on a fire, and they carry crews and gear into places a truck cannot reach.

The scale is large. Across Canada, over 8,000 wildland fires occur each year and burn an average of more than 2.1 million hectares (Natural Resources Canada). Aircraft are one tool in that fight. They rarely put a fire out alone, but they buy time for ground crews and keep flames off communities.

Provinces own most of the response. The agencies that fight these fires own the majority of firefighting capacity in Canada (Public Safety Canada).

Who decides how a wildfire gets fought?

The decision is local. Wildland fire management in Canada is a provincial and territorial responsibility. Provincial and territorial governments, working with local authorities, decide whether to and how to control wildland fires within their jurisdictions (Natural Resources Canada).

One body coordinates across provinces. The Canadian Interagency Forest Fire Centre (CIFFC) helps with the exchange of wildland fire fighting personnel, aircraft, equipment, and information between jurisdictions (Natural Resources Canada). CIFFC is a not-for-profit corporation owned and operated by the federal, provincial, and territorial wildland fire management agencies, and the provinces, territories, and local authorities remain primarily responsible for wildfire preparedness and response in their jurisdictions (Public Safety Canada).

So a province sets the plan for its own fires. CIFFC moves people and machines between provinces when one is stretched thin.

Where do the helicopters come from?

In Saskatchewan, the wildfire agency is the Saskatchewan Public Safety Agency (SPSA). It owns and operates a fleet of 10 air tankers and seven bird-dog aircraft: three Convair 580A and one Q400AT land-based air tankers, six CL-215T turbine water-scooping air tankers, and seven smaller bird-dog aircraft (Government of Saskatchewan).

Note what is not on that list. Helicopters are not part of the owned fleet. The SPSA depends on the contracting of helicopters and fixed-wing aircraft from commercial air carriers to help detect and suppress wildfires on a long or short-term basis (Government of Saskatchewan). The agency's owned equipment is supplemented by hired aircraft and services, including helicopters, fixed-wing aircraft, heavy equipment, and transportation (Government of Saskatchewan).

Because the helicopters are contracted, they typically fly in their operator's livery rather than a government one.

How do aircraft work together over a fire?

Aerial firefighting is a group effort, not a lone aircraft. In SPSA operations, the seven bird-dog aircraft guide the tankers and direct other air traffic over and near the wildfire (Government of Saskatchewan).

A tanker group runs eight people. It is typically staffed by five pilots, two aircraft maintenance engineers, and one air attack officer (Government of Saskatchewan). The air attack officer runs the picture from above. The bird-dog clears the airspace. The tankers and helicopters fly their runs in turn. Order over a fire is what keeps the drops accurate and the aircraft apart.

How do provinces back each other up?

When a fire season runs hot, demand can outrun any one jurisdiction's aircraft, so agencies lend and borrow.

That trade runs through CIFFC, the mechanism by which a province like Saskatchewan can request or lend helicopters and crews during busy fire seasons (Natural Resources Canada). Because CIFFC is owned and operated by the federal, provincial, and territorial agencies themselves, it can also coordinate international assistance when Canadian capacity is fully committed (Public Safety Canada). A quiet province sends its tankers and crews to a burning one, then gets the favour returned.

What does it look like from the cockpit?

The contracted helicopter, the bird-dog clearing the airspace, the air attack officer calling the runs (Government of Saskatchewan): all of it is built to put water on the fire and hold it off the towns. If you want to feel what a scoop and a drop are like over northern Saskatchewan, you can fly one yourself.

Frequently asked

Does Saskatchewan own its firefighting helicopters?
No. The Saskatchewan Public Safety Agency owns 10 air tankers and seven bird-dog aircraft, but not helicopters. It contracts helicopters and fixed-wing aircraft from commercial air carriers to help detect and suppress wildfires on a long or short-term basis (Government of Saskatchewan).
Who decides how a wildfire in Canada gets fought?
Provincial and territorial governments, working with local authorities, decide whether to and how to control wildland fires within their jurisdictions. Wildland fire management is a provincial and territorial responsibility (Natural Resources Canada).
What does a bird-dog aircraft do?
In SPSA operations the bird-dog aircraft guide the tankers and direct other air traffic over and near the wildfire, keeping the airspace ordered so drops stay accurate and aircraft stay apart (Government of Saskatchewan).
How do provinces share aircraft during a bad fire season?
Through the Canadian Interagency Forest Fire Centre (CIFFC), which helps exchange wildland fire fighting personnel, aircraft, equipment, and information between jurisdictions. It is owned and operated by the federal, provincial, and territorial agencies and can also coordinate international assistance (Natural Resources Canada; Public Safety Canada).
How big is Canada's wildfire problem each year?
Across the country, over 8,000 wildland fires occur annually and burn an average of more than 2.1 million hectares (Natural Resources Canada).
