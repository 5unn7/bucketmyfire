## The thing that actually burns houses down

Picture a wildfire and you picture a wall of flame rolling through the trees. But that wall is not what usually takes a house. Embers are.

Wind throws burning embers well ahead of the fire. They rain down on roofs, pile into gutters, blow under decks, and slip through vents. If they land on something dry and ready to burn, the house lights up long before the main fire arrives. Sometimes the fire front never reaches the building at all. An ember finds the woodpile against the wall, and that is enough.

That changes the job. You are not trying to hold back a wall of flame. You are trying to make sure that when embers land, they have nothing to catch. That is what clearing the space around your home does, and most of it is a weekend of work.

## Start at the wall: the first 1.5 metres

The strip right against the house is where your effort pays off most. Treat the first 1.5 metres around every wall as a no-fuel zone.

Move out anything that burns:

- Firewood and lumber. Never stack it against the siding.
- Bark mulch and dry leaves in the beds. Swap mulch here for gravel or bare soil.
- Deck furniture, doormats, brooms, and planters with dry plants.
- Propane bottles, gas cans, and the recycling bin.

If a deck wraps the house, clear out everything stored underneath it and screen the gap so embers cannot blow in. This strip is small and the work is cheap, which is exactly why it is the first thing to do.

## Clean the roof and gutters

Your roof is a giant landing pad for embers, and your gutters are a tray to collect kindling. A gutter full of dry conifer needles is one of the most reliable ways for a house to catch.

So before the season:

- Sweep the roof clear of needles and leaves, especially in the valleys.
- Clean every gutter out by hand.
- Cut back any branch that hangs over the roof or touches the chimney.
- Screen vents and openings with fine metal mesh so embers cannot get into the attic or crawlspace.

This is dull work and it is some of the most valuable you will do.

## Work outward in zones

Past the wall, think in widening rings and let the effort taper.

**The yard, out to about 10 metres.** Keep grass cut short and green where you can. Break up the fuel: leave space between shrubs, pull bushes away from windows, and store the woodpile out here, not by the house. A gap in the fuel is a gap the fire has to jump.

**The edge, beyond that.** You are not clearing the forest, just thinning it. Prune the low branches off trees so a ground fire cannot climb into the crowns. Leave space between treetops so fire cannot run from one to the next. Clear deadfall and the worst of the dry brush.

The goal across all of it is the same. Nowhere for a fire to build, and nothing easy for an ember to start.

## Do it before the smoke

The best time for all of this is a cool weekend long before any fire is near, when you can take your time and do it well. By the time there is smoke on the horizon, the window for yard work has closed and the only job left is leaving.

Pair a cleared yard with a packed go-bag and a written plan, and you have covered the three things that protect a household most: the house hardened, the kit ready, and the exit decided.
