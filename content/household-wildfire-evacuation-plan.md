## Why a written plan beats a good memory

When a wildfire is bearing down, no one thinks clearly. Adrenaline narrows your focus and good intentions evaporate. A plan you wrote on a calm day does the thinking for you.

So make it real and make it simple. One page, on the fridge, that anyone in the house can read and follow. If it does not fit on a page, it is too complicated to use on the worst day of the year.

## The five things every plan needs

A solid household plan answers five questions. Write the answers down.

1. **What is our trigger to leave?** A warning level, a smell of smoke, a glow on the ridge. Decide the line, then honour it.
2. **Which two ways do we drive out?** A main route and a backup in a different direction.
3. **Where do we meet?** One spot near home for a fast exit, and one outside the area in case we cannot get back.
4. **Who is our out-of-area contact?** One person everyone calls to check in.
5. **Who grabs what?** Name who takes the kids, the pets, the medications, and the go-bags.

That is the whole plan. Everything else is detail.

## Two ways out

A wildfire does not care about your usual route. It can close your road with flame, smoke, or a wall of stopped cars. So pick two ways out of your neighbourhood that head in different directions.

Then drive both. A route you have driven once is far easier to follow in the dark, through smoke, with your heart pounding. Note where they connect to a main highway away from the bush.

If you live at the end of a single road, your plan leans even harder on leaving early, while that one road is still clear.

## A meeting place and a contact

Households get separated. Someone is at work, a kid is at a friend's, you leave in two vehicles and lose each other in traffic. Plan for it.

Pick two meeting places: one close to home for a quick reunion, and one well outside the area for when you cannot get back. A relative's house, a community centre, a known parking lot.

Then name one out-of-area contact. Phones near a fire get overwhelmed fast, but a call to someone a province away often goes through. Everyone in the household checks in with that one person, who becomes the hub that knows where everyone is.

## Plan for pets and livestock

Animals take time, and time is the thing you run out of. Decide now.

For pets: who carries the carrier, where the leash lives, and which go-bag holds their food. Never leave a pet behind hoping to come back. You may not be allowed to.

For livestock and large animals, the math is harder and the answer is earlier. Moving horses or cattle is slow, so a livestock plan means leaving on the first warning, not the last. Line up a trailer, a destination, and a person to help, long before a fire is near. If you truly cannot move them in time, opening gates to a grazed or cleared field can give them a chance.

## Practice once, then relax

Walk the household through the plan once a year, before the season starts. Who grabs whom, where you meet, which way you drive. Five minutes turns a frantic scramble into something close to routine.

Pin the page to the fridge, keep the go-bags by the door, and you have done the part of preparedness that saves lives: the deciding, done early, while it was still easy.
