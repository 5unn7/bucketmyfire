## The one rule that matters: leave early

If you remember nothing else, remember this. Leave early. The people who get hurt are almost always the ones who waited to see how bad it would get.

A wildfire does not arrive on a schedule. Wind shifts, the fire jumps a road, and the calm afternoon becomes a wall of smoke in minutes. By the time leaving feels urgent, the roads are often already full and the air is hard to drive through. The safe move is to be gone before that point.

So set a trigger now, while it is easy to think clearly. A trigger is a line you decide in advance: a warning level, a smell of smoke, a glow on the ridge. When you hit it, you go. No debate, no one last thing.

## Know the warning levels

Most places use a simple set of stages. The names differ, the meaning does not.

- **Get ready.** A fire is in the area. Pack the car, fuel up, and keep your phone and alerts on.
- **Get set.** Conditions are worsening. Leave now if anyone in the house is slow to move, has health needs, or you have animals to load.
- **Go.** Leave immediately by your planned route. Do not stop to gather more.

Treat "get ready" as your cue to act, not your cue to relax. The earlier you move, the more choices you have.

## Harden the house in your last minutes

If you have time before you leave, spend it making the house harder for embers to catch, not packing photo albums. Embers, not the wall of flame, start most house fires. They blow ahead of the fire and land in anything dry.

Quick actions, in rough order of value:

- Move anything that burns away from the walls: firewood, deck furniture, doormats, propane bottles, recycling.
- Close all windows, doors, and vents. Pull flammable curtains back from the glass.
- Bring a hose to the front and leave it connected. Fill buckets, sinks, and tubs with water.
- Turn on outside lights so crews can see the house through smoke.
- Shut off the gas at the meter if you know how and have time.

None of this is worth missing your window to leave. If the trigger says go, go.

## Load the car the smart way

Back the car into the driveway, nose out, windows up, with the keys in it. You want to be able to drive away in seconds without a three-point turn in the dark.

Put the go-bag, medications, documents, and pets in first. Then people. Dress everyone in long sleeves, long pants, closed shoes, and a hat. Synthetic fabrics melt, so choose cotton or wool if you can.

Keep the fuel tank at least half full through fire season. A full tank is one less stop on the way out.

## If you cannot get out

Sometimes the window closes before you make it. If the road is full of flame or smoke you cannot see through, do not drive into it. That is the most dangerous thing you can do.

Instead, find the most cleared space you can: a large parking lot, a green field, a building with the doors and windows shut. Get low, cover your mouth, and keep something between you and the radiant heat. Call emergency services, tell them exactly where you are, and stay on the line.

The fire front usually passes in a matter of minutes. Surviving those minutes is the whole job.

## Build the plan before the season

Every action above is easier if you decided it in advance. Pick your trigger, pack your go-bag, agree on two ways out of the neighbourhood, and walk your household through it once. A fire day is not the time to be making these calls for the first time.
