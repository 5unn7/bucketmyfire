## You do not need a fire next door

Wildfire smoke travels. A fire burning hundreds of kilometres away can turn your sky brown and your air harsh for days. You can be nowhere near a flame and still spend a week breathing the worst air of the year.

That is why smoke is its own preparedness problem, separate from evacuation. The fire may never threaten your home, but the smoke will still find you. The good news is that protecting your indoor air is cheap, simple, and mostly about keeping the bad air out and cleaning the air you have.

## Close the house up

When the air outside turns smoky, your first move is to seal the house and keep it sealed.

- Close all the windows and doors, and keep them closed.
- Shut off anything that pulls outside air in, like a whole-house fan or a fresh-air intake.
- If your furnace or air conditioner has a fresh-air setting, switch it to recirculate so it cools or heats the air already inside.
- Use a good filter in the furnace and change it more often than usual during a smoke event.

The aim is simple. Make the house a sealed box, then clean the air trapped inside it.

## Do not add more smoke indoors

Once the house is closed, do not undo it by making smoke or dust inside. During a smoke event, avoid:

- Burning candles, incense, or anything in a fireplace or wood stove.
- Frying or broiling food, which throws particles into the air.
- Vacuuming, which stirs settled dust back up, unless your vacuum has a sealed fine filter.
- Smoking indoors.

Every one of those adds to the particle load in a house you are trying to keep clean.

## Build a clean-air room

You do not need to purify the whole house. Pick one room and make it your clean-air room, usually a bedroom you can close off, ideally with few windows.

In that room:

- Run a portable air purifier sized for the space, if you have one.
- Keep the door shut and spend the smokiest hours in there, especially for sleeping.
- Bring in water, chargers, medications, and whatever keeps the household comfortable for a long stretch.

This is the room where the most vulnerable people in the house ride out the worst air: kids, older adults, anyone with asthma or a heart condition.

## The box-fan filter

No purifier on hand? Build one. It is a well-known trick and it works.

Take a clean furnace filter and tape it flat against the back of a box fan, so the fan pulls air through the filter. Stand it in your clean-air room and run it on the side that draws air in through the filter. The fan pushes a lot of air through the filter, and the filter catches a real share of the smoke particles.

It is not a lab-grade machine, but it is cheap, it uses parts you may already have, and in one closed room it makes a noticeable difference.

## Watch the people, and the air

Keep an eye on how everyone feels. Scratchy throat and stinging eyes are common and ease once the air clears. But trouble breathing, a tight chest, or a pounding heart are reasons to get medical help, especially for anyone with a heart or lung condition.

Check your local air-quality reports through the day. When the outdoor air finally improves, open the house up during that clear window to flush out whatever smoke crept in. Then close it back up if the smoke returns.

A few cheap supplies and a sealed room turn a brutal smoke week into something your household can simply wait out.
